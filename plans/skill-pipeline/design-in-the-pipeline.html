Two kinds of design get conflated — this is the most useful distinction

Design thinking

Cross-cutting · belongs in every stage

The lens, not the artefact. Surfacing the journey. Naming the moments-of-truth. Calling the IA risks. Flagging accessibility from day one. Identifying which stakeholders feel which pain.

  • Maps user journeys before we commit to a UI
  • Identifies IA / surface-parity decisions early
  • Surfaces failure-mode UX risks
  • Calls out a11y constraints that will shape the spec

Lives in: Discovery doc · PRD Design pointers · Critique rows

Design artefacts

Staged · each fidelity has a place

The deliverables. Sketches, journey maps, wireframes, hi-fi mockups, prototypes. Each fidelity belongs at a specific stage — pulling them earlier is where over-spec and stakeholder-anchoring start.

  • Sketches and journey maps — discovery
  • Constraint-language — PRD (Design pointers / a11y)
  • Wireframes — stage 06, after spec_ready
  • Hi-fi mocks and prototypes — stage 06, in coordination with build

Lives in: discovery sketches · Figma · Claude Design wireframe mode

Design’s role per stage

# Stage Design’s role here Artefact at this stage Deliberately NOT
01 Idea Absent. The idea is one paragraph — design has no input here yet. — sketches, mockups
02 Discovery Surface the user journey. Identify IA risks and surface-parity decisions. Flag a11y constraints that will shape requirements. Identify design stakeholders. written narrative + rough sketches in the discovery doc · journey notes · IA risk callouts wireframes, hi-fi mockups
03 PRD Set design constraints. Author the Design pointers, Failure-mode UX, and A11y requirements sections of the bond_v1 PRD. These constrain what the wireframe must achieve. PRD sections: Design pointers · Failure-mode UX · A11y requirements wireframes, screen-by-screen mocks
04 Critique Defend design choices against demo risk and cohesion. Catch over-engineering early via the product-critique “Cohesion vs craft” row and the engineering-critique “A11y first-class” row. flagged must-fixes in the critique log new design work, rework before fold
05 spec_ready Hand-shake. Design constraints in PRD are signed off. Frontmatter flips: design_pass_status: pending. PRD frontmatter pipeline state —
06 Design Expression. With the PRD in hand, design iterates on form without re-litigating substance. Wireframes follow the slice plan — slice 1 wireframes first, then thicker slices. wireframes · prototypes · hi-fi mocks (Claude Design / Figma) new requirements, scope additions
07 Build handoff Realisation. Design pairs with engineering to defend pixel intent without freezing the implementation. Slice 1 ships rough; design polish lands incrementally. shipped UI · design QA notes new requirements, scope additions

The fidelity trap — named, not blamed

Pattern

The fidelity trap

A discovery moment that should produce a journey map produces a wireframe instead. The wireframe is shown to stakeholders. Stakeholders anchor on a specific UI. Sunk-cost kicks in. Changing course feels expensive even when the problem hasn’t been validated. Engineering ends up implementing the mockup — not the spec.

This isn’t a designer flaw — it’s a systems flaw. Designers default to fidelity because fidelity is what the tools optimise for. Without a typed role per stage, the tool wins.

  1. Symptom: high-fidelity Figma in week one of discovery
  2. Symptom: design decisions made before slice 1 is named
  3. Symptom: requirements that read like UI specs

Built-in defences

What the pipeline catches

  1. Cohesion vs craft — /critique-product-custom row 4 catches “artefact ships craft without changing an observable user moment.” That’s the literal definition of over-engineering, named and checked every PRD.
  2. First-demo risk — /critique-product-custom row 2 forces design choices to defend against “the most embarrassing thing that could happen in the room” on a real demo. Craft must justify itself against viability.
  3. Walking skeleton — slice 1 must demo end-to-end, however rough. Design can’t hide behind “the design isn’t ready yet.” Slice 1 ships ugly. This is the strongest single anti-over-spec mechanism.

Why this calibration fits Wyzetalk

Requirements-first works here

B2B frontline employee engagement

ICP is well-constrained. Jobs-to-be-done are well-known (HR self-service, comms, SHEQ, recognition). Differentiation is in functional fit and operational reliability — not aesthetic novelty. The risk in this category is over-spec’ing details that don’t move a user moment, not under-designing.

Where requirements-first fails

Consumer / design-led products

Design-led discovery is correct for product categories where the design is the differentiator — consumer notes apps, design tools, content products. Wyzetalk isn’t in that category. Don’t adopt a framework calibrated for that category here.

Handoff language — lift verbatim

If asked: “Where does design fit?”

Design has a typed role in every stage of this pipeline — not just stage 06. In discovery, design surfaces the journey and the IA risks in writing. In the PRD, design sets constraints. In critique, design defends choices against demo risk and cohesion. Wireframes live in stage 06 because that’s where they earn their keep.

If asked: “But design dictates what the tech does”

Design constrains the tech — and the PRD captures those constraints in Design pointers. Engineering also constrains design. Both feed the spec. The walking skeleton in slice 1 forces both sides to ship something rough end-to-end before either side polishes — that’s the discipline.

Glossary — for any reader who isn’t fluent

a11y accessibility
The number 11 stands for the eleven letters between “a” and “y.” Covers screen-reader compatibility, keyboard navigation, colour contrast, focus management, the lang attribute on translated content, and tap-target sizes. Both a usability and a regulatory concern.
IA information architecture
How content is structured, labelled, organised, and navigated. The “where does this live, what’s it called, how do users find it” lens. IA decisions are high-leverage and hard to reverse — which is why design-thinking belongs in discovery for those.
JTBD jobs to be done
Framework where you describe what users hire your product to do, in functional terms. “Help me know I got paid correctly” is a JTBD. “A payslip screen” is a solution.
walking skeleton
A first end-to-end vertical slice that demos the full system, however rough — data layer to UI, on the happy path. The opposite of a horizontal layer-by-layer build. Term from agile / Cockburn.
vertical slice
A thin end-to-end implementation that touches every layer needed to deliver a single user-visible outcome. Each slice is independently demoable. Slices stack — later slices thicken or extend earlier ones.
Design pointers
A required section of the bond_v1 PRD shape. Captures the design constraints the wireframe must honour: key journeys, failure-mode UX, a11y requirements, copy tone. Not wireframes — constraints.
Failure-mode UX
What the user sees when the happy path doesn’t work. Vendor outage. Network failure. Permission denied. Stale cache. A required subsection of Design pointers when the PRD has external dependencies.
Critique loop
After PRD authoring, the product and engineering critique skills run. Must-fixes loop back to the PRD author. Two passes is normal. Three is a smell. Loop closes when both sign off — PRD flips to spec_ready.

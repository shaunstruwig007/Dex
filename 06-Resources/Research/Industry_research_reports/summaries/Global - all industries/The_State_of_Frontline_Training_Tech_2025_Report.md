Methodology
This report is based on insights gathered from over 
1,200 respondents. We sourced responses from leaders 
working in direct and indirect proximity to the frontline, 
allowing us to capture the perspectives across those 
involved in training delivery, technology deployment, 
and frontline management.
Our respondents included both frontline leaders and 
workers. Frontline leaders represented roles in L&D, HR, 
operations, and area management. Frontline workers 
covered a range of roles, from cashiers, production 
workers, and machine operators to truck drivers, front 
desk workers, and hospitality staff like housekeeping, 
bar, and kitchen staff.
To add nuance to the dataset, we employed both 
quantitative and qualitative methods. Using a 
combination of single and multiple-choice questions 
alongside open-text responses, we gathered both 
structured data and richer, narrative insights that 
highlight frontline experiences and perspectives.
Our goal was to explore key questions around current 
technology use, training practices, and organizational 
alignment with frontline needs. We also examined the 
role of AI in frontline operations—how frequently it’s 
being adopted, in what capacities, and with what level 
of support.

Key Findings
Training is foundational to business success 
1
Both frontline leaders and workers overwhelmingly agree that effective training is crucial for driving key business outcomes such as employee 
satisfaction, retention, performance, and productivity. Frontline leaders believe training significantly impacts customer experience (93%), 
retention (89%) and workforce performance and productivity (91%). 83% of frontline workers also flag training investment as vital in a 
prospective employer.
Despite this mutual recognition, a significant gap exists between the perceived value of training and the quality of its current implementation, 
with 52% of frontline workers indicating they don’t retain information well from training due to infrequent reinforcement.

Technology is pivotal, but access issues persist
5
2
Frontline workers rely heavily on technology in their day-to-day tasks, with 90% reporting that they are “somewhat reliant” or “very 
reliant” on technology. However, discrepancies exist between the technology that frontline leaders report as available and what workers 
report primarily using. While 60% of frontline workers prefer using mobile devices for work-related tasks, only 4% currently access work-
related software via their own devices.
Along with devices, frontline workers are demanding streamlined entry points into training, with 52% of frontline workers expressing a 
preference for having all work-related software “bundled together in one app or as few locations as possible” , vs. spread across multiple 
platforms. Additionally, nearly 50% of frontline leaders identify “complex” access as a primary barrier to tech use, indicating that overly 
complicated or inaccessible technology continues to hinder frontline productivity and training effectiveness.

Key Findings
AI is a promising but underutilized tool3
While there is growing awareness of AI’s potential to streamline training creation and delivery, adoption rates remain low. Only 22% of 
frontline leaders report company-sanctioned use of AI, while an additional 27% use it informally, without formal support.
Barriers include a lack of leadership commitment (30%), internal resistance (18%), and limited formal training, with 26% indicating they 
“wouldn’t know where to start” . These factors are slowing AI’s widespread use in frontline training environments, even as 64% of frontline 
leaders express a desire to use AI more frequently.

Key Findings
Training formats and delivery methods are not frontline-friendly4
Despite the shift towards a more tech-savvy workforce, many businesses still rely on long, in-
person training sessions that are poorly suited to the fast-moving, mobile nature of frontline 
work. The majority of training sessions last between 30 minutes and multiple hours, and only 
1% of respondents report sessions under 5 minutes. 
Both frontline leaders and workers are calling for more frequent, shorter, and digitally 
delivered training sessions, with 63% of frontline workers stating they would prefer shorter 
sessions and 39% expressing a desire for more engaging and interactive training formats.

The business impact of training  
T raining’s trickle-down effect on organizations is far-reaching, with the 
potential to influence each problem area above identified by leaders.
Onboarding is the first training touchpoint an employee will have with 
your organization, and lays the foundation for employee experience at 
your company – it’s highly influential for priming frontline workers for 
either longer-term tenure, or premature turnover. 
If empowered by training from the onboarding stage and beyond, frontline 
workers are able to do their jobs better. For customer-facing employees 
as in retail or hospitality, this translates to higher customer satisfaction.
As the public face of the brand, frontline employees must be equipped to 
handle a variety of customer interactions, from routine inquiries to more 
complex problem-solving scenarios. Leaders acknowledge this, with data 
revealing that 93% of frontline leaders believe employee training impacts

Training’s influence on retention   
Employee turnover is multi-faceted, and influenced by multiple factors – from dissatisfaction with working conditions, to lack of managerial support 
and basic technical knowhow, to little opportunity to grow and develop on the job. 
89% of leaders shared the belief that better training could help lower turnover rates beyond current levels. T raining’s retention-influencing impact 
lies in its ability to shape and improve the employee experience. By providing employees with a blueprint for success, frontline workers can work more 
efficiently, safely, achieve greater recognition and ultimately, higher earnings. This in turn shapes the employees’ perception of the company. 
Leaders in fact ranked ‘more training to improve day-to-day productivity and performance’ as their most potent retention strategy, more so than any 
other, ahead of even perks and company benefits, career progression or if their organization was more values-aligned with employees.

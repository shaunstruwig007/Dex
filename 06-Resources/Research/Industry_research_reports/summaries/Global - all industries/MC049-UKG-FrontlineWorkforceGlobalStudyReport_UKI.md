Perspectives from the 
Frontline Workforce:  
A UKG Global Study
October 2024
This report details findings from a 2024 global study on the frontline 
workforce, commissioned by UKG and Workplace Intelligence and 
conducted by Walr. It features perspectives from nearly 13,000 frontline 
employees and leaders on a range of topics that drive engagement, 
recruiting, and retention, from trust, support, and benefits to work-
life balance, flexibility, and recognition. For the purposes of this study, 
UKG defines a frontline employee as someone who directly engages 
with customers, manufacturers products, or delivers services. For some 
industries, customers may be defined as patients, students, citizens, 
guests, etc. Typically, a frontline employee must be physically present in a 
specific place or at a specific time to perform their job.

Feeling Overworked and Underappreciated
“Return to office” continues to be highly debated among the laptop-toting workforce. Due to 
the nature of their roles, however, nearly all frontline employees must be physically present at a 
specific work location to do their jobs. Thus, the frontline debate centers on how to best support 
these employees in the workplace.
Across industries, our study finds frontline employees 
overwhelmingly crave greater flexibility at work, more 
time off, and even increased opportunities for voluntary 
overtime (OT). One workplace concern that the frontline 
workforce shares with corporate employees, though, is the 
high levels of burnout they experience on the job. Indeed, 
three-quarters of frontline employees globally in our study 
report feeling burned out, including one-quarter who say 
they’re “always” or “often” burned out at work.
Moreover, 60% of frontline employees say they’re pressured 
to be more productive without additional compensation, 
65% feel they have to work long hours in order to make 
ends meet, and more than 1 in 10 (12%) say they’ve never 
received a pay raise. Adding proverbial bruises to the 
burnout, nearly 1 in 5 frontline employees (19%) say they’re 
never recognised by their manager. 
These less-than-ideal conditions have put many frontline 
employees on the brink of quitting their jobs. 
Over three-quarters of frontline 
employees in healthcare (77%) say they’re 
burned out at work, and 1 in 4 (25%) say 
they’re never recognised by their manager 
— signaling that challenges for these 
essential employees remain years after 
the pandemic’s peak.

Trading More Money for More Time
Even though 64% of frontline employees globally report living paycheck to paycheck, and 55% say they rely 
on OT earnings to cover their monthly expenses, for more than half of the frontline workforce, making more 
money isn’t always as valuable as having more quality time away from the workplace.
For instance, our study found that 53% of frontline 
employees would rather have more vacation time than 
receive a pay increase. Furthermore, 27% say they’d give 
up a promotion, and 19% would give up a portion of their 
salary/wages, for an extra week of paid time off (PTO) 
each year. Of note, not everyone across the frontline 
shares these sentiments: 30% of frontline employees 
globally say they wouldn’t give up anything  for an extra 
week of PTO annually.
What’s driving this desire for more downtime? About two-
thirds of frontline employees globally (64%) say they’re not 
able to take as much time off work as they’d like to, with 
59% saying they don’t have enough time in the day to enjoy 
their life.
And while most employees can’t work from home, they 
don’t always leave work at the workplace, either.

Craving Greater

Our study finds work-life balance/flexibility 
and work schedule (tied at 55%) trail only pay/
compensation for the most important factors 
frontline employees consider when choosing an 
employer. Looking across the global landscape, 
providing the frontline workforce with more flexible 
work options (or any at all) is a key opportunity for 
enhancing the employee experience.
Currently, a little over half of organisations with frontline 
employees (54%) offer flexible hours/self-scheduling, 
according to our study. Other flexible scheduling practices 
are catching on, too, including shift trading with co-workers 
(41%), part-time work with no loss of benefits (28%), 
compressed workweeks (27%), and job sharing (18%). 
However, there’s still room for growth: more than 1 in 10 
frontline leaders (14%) say their organisation doesn’t offer 
any of these flexible work arrangements.

Recognising the Need for Recognition
Recognising and rewarding frontline employees’ meaningful contributions to the business starts with 
managers, and it extends across the organisation. However, our study reveals nearly 1 in 5 frontline 
employees globally (19%) say they’re never recognised by their manager. 
As for how often frontline employees would like to receive 
praise from their managers, the desired frequency varies 
across the workforce: 22% of employees prefer weekly 
recognition, 34% want it monthly, 23% think quarterly 
suffices, 9% say semiannually, and 8% believe being 
recognised once a year is enough for them.
Likewise, the preferred recognition method looks different 
across the frontline workforce. Besides a pay raise or 
promotion, the top ways frontline employees want to be 
recognised include a cash bonus (66%), PTO (55%), gift 
cards (42%), a positive rating on their performance review 
(35%), and free meals (26%).
There is a slight disconnect, however, among how the 
frontline prefers to be recognised and how organisations 
currently provide employee recognition.

Abstract
This paper assessed the impact of mining operations on the health of mining workers. Additionally, the challenges faced by 
mine workers due to health issues and provisions arranged by mines for workers’ healthcare were analysed from the social 
work and human rights perspectives. A cross-sectional study was conducted in a mineral-rich district of Odisha, India, by 
covering 257 households consisting of 296 mine workers of both public and private sector mines. The result showed mining 
operations had a critical negative environmental and occupational health impact on workers. Diseases like colds, cough, 
fever, bronchitis, jaundice, acute respiratory infection, gallbladder stone, blood pressure, skin disease, tuberculosis, and joint 
pain occurred frequently with greater intensity along with the deformity and death caused by mining accidents. Against the 
losses, the workers hardly receive compensation as mandated by Mining Act, 1952 and Director General of Mines Safety 
guidelines due to their lack of information, casual job contracts, poor implementation of mining law, lack of monitoring of 
mining activities by the government, and the profit-maximizing behaviour of the mines. In this context, the study followed the 
human rights and workers’ right principles and provisions of United Nations-Human Right Council, World Health Organi-
zation, International Labour Organization, and the Mining Act, 1952, and recommends a set of principles, i.e. employment 
of an adequate number of qualified health social workers/human right professionals both at micro and macro levels, and 
formulates an appropriate plan to ensure good health and well-being to mining workers. Additionally, strict implementation 
of mining laws, periodic social and environmental impact assessment, permanent job contracts to workers, social protection 
and security of workers, and creation of adequate health infrastructure in the mining locality is essential.

Introduction
Mining operations around the world are believed to be 
one of the most perilous activities conducted in an acci-
dent and disease-prone environment (Das & Singh, 2011; 
Nandi et al., 2015). The socioeconomic and environmental 
costs of mining activities are a major social concern vis-
à-vis social work intervention because they violate human 
rights by imposing a huge toll on workers and the peo -
ple living in the periphery (Donoghue, 2004; Rosenstock 
et al., 2006; Sahoo et al., 2022). Extraction and processing 
of mineral ores cause serious environmental issues such 
as dust emission, pollution (water, air, noise, and soil), 
biodiversity issues, ecosystem degradation, and ground-
water table depletion (Symon, 2007; Azad et al., 2015  
and Nandi et al., 2015), which result in numerous social, 
physiological, and physical health risks in the mining local-
ity. Additionally, occupational hazards like work-related 
diseases and accidents due to mining range from trivial to 
the loss of precious life (Donoghue, 2004 & Rosenstock 
et al., 2006). An International Labour Organization report 
reveals occupational accidents and work-related diseases  
cause more than 2.8 million fatalities globally every year

India produces five percent of the total minerals in the world  
and is ranked seventh globally for its abundance of min-
erals (Reichl et al., 2018). Along with massive mineral 
production, the issues related to mining workplace safety, 
workers’ security, and diseases are gigantic in the country 
(Nandi et al., 2015). To ensure healthcare, safety, security, 
and welfare of workers along with the lawful regulation of 
mining activities, the Government of India has been amend-
ing the Mines Act, 1952 periodically (Mining Act, 1952). 
The Director General of Mines Safety (DGMS) acts as the 
apex body in India to monitor and regulate the entire pro-
cess (DGMS, 2015). However, the enforcement could not 
be done appropriately due to a number of reasons like poor 
monitoring by DGMS, low level of literacy and awareness 
among mining workers, and profit-making behaviour of 
mine owners (Lahiri-Dutt, 1999). As a result, the workers 
could not receive the basic healthcare, safety, security, and  
other socioeconomic benefits as directed by the mining laws  
(Sahoo et al., 2022), which ultimately results in serious 
human right violation.
In this perspective, the present study aims to answer the 
questions related to health and occupational issues of min-
ing workers and the benefits received from mines. What 
are the different health issues faced by mining workers? 
Do mine owners follow the DGMS guidelines for work -
ers’ healthcare, safety, and welfare? Are the health issues 
and mining accidents addressed properly? Are the workers 
benefited appropriately as per law? Is there any difference 
between private and public mines in healthcare delivery and 
welfare of workers? To address all the above questions, the 
present study used primary data collected from 257 work -
ers’ households in the mining region of Kendujhar district, 
Odisha, India.
Review of Literature
Mineral extraction and processing is essential for poverty 
eradication, employment generation, infrastructure crea-
tion, economic development,  trade and commerce, and 
to strengthen the pecuniary status of a middle-income 
country like India (Atakora & Stenberg, 2020; Mishra, 
2009). As a result, since independence, the Indian mining 
industry has been expanding with increase in quantity and 
value of mineral production. Currently, India produces 95 
minerals that include fuel (4), metallic (10), non-metallic 
(23), atomic (3), and minor minerals (55) (GoI, 2022a). In 
2018–2019, the total value of mineral production in India 
reached Rs.144,883.77 crore, which accounts for 2.20% of 
the national gross domestic product (GDP) (GoI, 2022a). 
Besides GDP growth, massive production of mineral ores 
has strengthened international trade by expanding mineral 
exports by as much as Rs.165,492.42 crore and increased 
foreign currency reserves in India (DGCI&S, 2021). Addi-
tionally, mining activities play an important role in employ-
ment creation in the second largest populated country that 
generated employment for 20 lakh people during 2018–2019 
(GoI, 2022b).
In addition to all the above positive impacts, mining 
activities pose several negative effects on mine workers 
and people living in the periphery (Atakora & Stenberg, 
2020). Most of the mining activities take place in remote 
forest/hilly areas, which are home to aboriginal/tribal peo-
ple (Sahoo, 2015). Historically, tribes have been suffer -
ing from historic injustices due to sheer deprivation and 
exploitation by the superior communities (Maharatna, 
2011; UNDRIP, 2007). Deprivating tribes of their basic 
rights resulted in a critically low level of literacy rate, 
lack of awareness, and poor participation of tribals in the 
development process. Additionally, displacement of tribes 
from their homeland, massive forest clearance, and exten-
sive acquisition and damage of agricultural land for mining 
activities (Nathan, 2009; Sahu, 2008) accelerate the level 
of backwardness and marginalization among the tribes by 
creating various social, economic, and environmental issues  
in the locality (Fisher, 2007).
Other than social issues, mining operations cause mas-
sive environmental hazards like dust emission, pollution 
(water, air, noise, and soil), biodiversity issues, ecosystem 
degradation, and groundwater table depletion (Azad et al., 
2015; Nandi et al., 2015) in the mining locality. Environ-
mental issues created by mines directly affect the traditional

Apart from physical health hazards, social issues like cor-

violence, and crime are the other major social problems in 
mining areas, which increases harsh insecurity among work-
ers and the local people (Bansah et al., 2018; Setyaningrum 
et al., 2022). Against the issues, i.e. health hazards, safety 
problems, insecurities, and other socioeconomic losses 
caused by mining operations, neither the local people nor 
mine workers get adequate compensation/support.

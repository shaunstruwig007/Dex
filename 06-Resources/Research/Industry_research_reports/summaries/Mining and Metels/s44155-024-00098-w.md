The occupational syndemics of miners in South Africa
Nicola Bulled1  · Merrill Singer2 
Received: 6 May 2024 / Accepted: 23 July 2024
© The Author(s) 2024  OPEN
Abstract
Occupational exposures in the large industrial mining sector contributed significantly to South Africa’s high excess 
death rate due to COVID-19. Historically poor work-protection oversight has perpetuated centuries of risky labor and 
living conditions within the industry, driving high levels of disease co-morbidities, and supporting enduring social 
vulnerabilities. In this paper, we offer a syndemic lens to consider the clustering of adversely interacting diseases among 
mineworkers in South Africa, drawing attention to the complex occupational health crisis and the need to move beyond 
simply reporting individual diseases or comorbidities among this population. The physically demanding and dangerous 
working conditions, the lack of adequate changes to crowded and unsanitary working and living situations, the failure 
to meet social and labor plan targets, the continued precarious nature of working contracts and mines, and the limited 
access to robust healthcare reflect the historically exploitative nature of industrial mining in South Africa that places 
miners at increased risk for various syndemics. This assessment of the adverse interactions of diseases and socioeconomic 
and political conditions highlights the need for focused research and more follow-through in comprehensive occupational 
reforms.
Keywords Occupational health · COVID-19 · HIV · Mining · Syndemics · South Africa
1 Introduction
South Africa had the highest excess death rate due to COVID-19 on the African continent through 2022 [1 ]. Numerous 
inter-related phenomena likely accounted for this increased disease burden, including having a higher median age 
than other African countries with older individuals more at risk for COVID-19 mortality; more established long-term care 
facilities where COVID-19 disease outbreaks occurred; and better diagnostics and healthcare documentation, allowing 
for higher reporting rates [2]. High rates of unemployment and social and economic inequity also contributed [3, 4], with 
85% of the population relying on an overburdened and under-resourced public health sector that is staffed by only 30% 
of the country’s doctors [5, 6]. A high prevalence of disease comorbidities also increased morbidity and mortality, with 
the World Health Organization ranking South Africa first in the world for HIV (7,800,000 people living with HIV), 8th in 
the world for Tuberculosis (TB) with 3.3% of global cases [7 ], and a growing prevalence of non-communicable diseases 
including hypertension, diabetes, and obesity [8 ].
Non-healthcare occupational exposures also contributed significantly to South Africa’s high COVID-19 disease burden.

The nature of mine work including largely underground operations in narrow shafts with inadequate ventilation, 
close physical proximity, and high dust exposure provides optimal physical and social conditions for the spread of 
communicable diseases including COVID-19, TB, and HIV. Many of the noxious substances encountered in mines, such 
as coal dust, crushed silica, diesel exhaust, mercury, cadmium, and asbestos, are toxic to humans and responsible for 
many of the severe occupational lung and other health problems that are commonly experienced among mineworkers. 
In addition, stress due to the challenging working environment and/or managerial pressure to perform gives rise to a 
range of comorbid diseases that affect miners [13].
Beyond direct workplace exposure, industry policies which include low pay, migrant and contractual labor, and limited 
access to paid sick leave and flexible work schedules, make miners more vulnerable to both communicable and non-
communicable diseases. According to 2017 labor reports from the Department of Mineral Resources and Energy in South 
Africa, the average annual earnings of mine workers was 19,444 ZAR [14], 61.7% less than yearly earnings with a national 
minimum wage of 25.42 ZAR.1 This confluence of work-related exposures, existing chronic diseases, and industry policy, 
what has been called the “peculiarities of the South African mining industry” [15], led to increased risk for acquiring 
COVID-19 infection, greater vulnerability for severe acute outcomes, and a higher incidence of serious post-infection 
sequelae.
In this paper, we present a syndemic examination of the biological and social dynamics among industrial mineworkers 
in South Africa in the era of COVID-19. First, we present how the syndemics perspective offers a way to comprehensively 
consider the increased health risks presented by occupational settings. Second, we provide an overview of the health risks 
presented in South Africa’s mining industry considering historic and current labor practices and their consequent disease 
outcomes. Third, we argue how these labor practices support an occupational syndemic in South Africa consisting of a 
clustering of interacting diseases (silicosis, TB, HIV, and COVID-19) in socially, economically, and politically marginalized 
mine workers. In so doing, we use a syndemic lens to draw attention to the complex occupational health crisis endured 
by vulnerable workers globally and the need for comprehensive occupational reforms.
Our interest in this topic emerges from long-term engagements with HIV programming efforts in South Africa, Lesotho, 
and the US, where we have observed how social, political, and historical factors heighten the risk for interacting disease 
clusters in socially and economically marginalized populations. One of the authors, Merrill Singer, developed the concept 
of syndemics to explain the clustering of HIV, substance use, and violence in Latinos in inner-city Hartford, CT. Together, we 
have applied the syndemic framework to understand the complexity of childhood diarrhea, neglected tropical diseases, 
syringe-mediated infections, tick-borne diseases, black fungus/diabetes/COVID-19, and diabetes/COVID-19. Recently, we 
have considered the syndemics of occupational exposures, given higher rates of COVID-19 linked to occupational settings, 
including among long-haul truck drivers [16, 17], commercial fishers [18], migrant farmers [19], manufacturing, retail 
trade, and transportation and warehousing [20]. Data for this paper are drawn from disparate sources: published scientific 
literature; country, industry, and global health organization reports and surveys; and grey and periodical literature.

Syndemics theory provides a critical alternative to the concept of comorbidities and social determinants of disease by 
examining two types of pernicious interaction simultaneously: (1) coterminous disease interplay in which the whole health 
impact is pathologically greater than the sum of the individual linked diseases (i.e., detrimental disease synergy); and (2) the 
effects on disease clustering, disease interaction, and health vulnerability caused by unequal and unjust structural relations 
(i.e., biosocial health determinants), commonly expressed in risky working, living, and environmental conditions.
The objective of a syndemic lens is to understand the whole health picture and the alignment of its constituent parts. In 
the investigation of these issues, the syndemics approach addresses three primary questions: (1) how do diseases interact 
within and between bodies in ways that enhance harm? (2) How do social relations and conditions get “under the skin” and 
promote disease and disease interaction? and, (3) what are the best methods for preventing and mitigating syndemics? 
Through these questions “we will be able to better understand why and how two [or more] diseases come together to form 
co-occurring maladies within a specific time and place” [23].
Urged by the COVID-19 pandemic, scholars have increasingly considered syndemics as they relate to occupations [24]. 
For example, studies found that long-haul truck drivers are at high risk for COVID-19 syndemics given working conditions 
that involve long periods of inactivity, poor diets, limited access to healthcare and consequently poor chronic disease 
management, and high-stress [16, 17]. Commercial fishers are also at particularly high risk for COVID-19, given their physically 
demanding and dangerous working conditions, crowded working sites, monotony, isolation from family support, sleep 
deprivation, fatigue, economic uncertainty, and poor diets [18]. These conditions, “reflect the hierarchical structure and 
political economy of commercial fishing, stress the minds and bodies of fishers, and promote alcohol, tobacco, drug use as 
coping and pain-alleviating behaviors, as well as increased adipose tissue accumulation” [18]. While configurations of these 
factors vary by location, they collectively place fishers at increased risk for syndemics.
Studies of migrant Mexican agricultural laborers in the US indicate a two to three times higher rate of COVID-19 infections 
among farmworkers than the general population, given that the sector was deemed “essential” during lockdowns and the 
nature of agricultural labor and communal living makes it nearly impossible to engage in appropriate physical distancing [19]. 
Furthermore, farmworkers have historically been excluded from health and labor protections and a significant proportion 
of Mexican and Central American farmworkers in the US are undocumented and therefore unable or unwilling to access 
healthcare for fear of deportation. Already heavily burdened by high rates of interlinked social and biological conditions 
including restrictive immigration enforcement, poverty, food insecurity, stress, depression, anxiety, overweight, obesity, high 
blood pressure, and diabetes, Mexicans in the US, many of whom are migrant farm laborers became victims of COVID-19 
syndemics, facing some of the highest rates of morbidity and mortality of any demographic sub-group [19].
Given these examples, occupational syndemics are important to consider as social conditions that heighten disease 
risk in marginalized populations can be further accentuated in the workspace and compounded by industry-specific labor 
policies. We now consider the occupational conditions of mining in South Africa that contribute to excess disease morbidity 
and mortality among workers.

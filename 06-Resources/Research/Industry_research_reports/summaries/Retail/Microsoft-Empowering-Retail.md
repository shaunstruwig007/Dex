RETAIL ASSOCIATES — THE LIFELINE OF THE SECTOR RETAIL WORKFORCE 
TRANSFORMATION IN SOUTH AFRICA
With many disruptors dividing organizations’ attention, the world over, the future of work is fraught with many unknowns — 
where and how work will be done, how economic pressures will change job opportunities, and how skills shortages, climate 
concerns, and other social and environmental factors will impact business. Hybrid work, once thought to be a temporary 
means of enabling enterprises to continue business operations through the COVID-19 pandemic, has become a mainstay 
for our global future of work landscape. For example, 37% of back-office employees in the South African retail sector 
currently work remotely or in hybrid mode1. However, for one specific segment of workers — retail associates — remote 
or hybrid work is not an option.
Retail associates, or frontline workers in broader terms, are the staff that must be physically present at the store or facility 
to perform their job duties. They are often called the deskless workforce. Worldwide, an estimated 497 million frontline 
workers are employed in retail2. During the pandemic, they were the lifeline of the economy. Retail associates worked extra 
hours to make sure stores were open, warehouses were stocked, and materials were delivered to meet volatile demand. 
One major effect of the crisis on retail associates was the heightened risk to health and safety due to their close interaction 
with customers.
In the digital era, technology is empowering every aspect of work. Despite the size of the workforce and the importance of 
their work, retail associates have traditionally been underserved by technology, yet the challenges they face keep rising. As 
we enter the post-pandemic era, the way retail associates function in the store needs to be reimagined, with an emphasis 
on fostering human-machine collaboration, enabling new skills and worker experiences, and supporting a workplace that 
blends physical and digital tools.
To understand the trends in this strategic segment, IDC conducted a survey among key stakeholders in South Africa’s retail 
sector — retail associates, retail managers, and HR managers. This report presents the key findings from this study, focusing 
on the key challenges retail associates face and how technology can address these challenges and help retail associates 
realize their full potential.

Retailers in South Africa are pursuing work transformation efforts among their frontline workers, driven by several external 
and internal factors. An IDC survey revealed evolving customer expectations to be the biggest driver of this transformation 
for most organizations. In the retail industry, what started as the winds of change at the onset of the pandemic evolved into 
a full-on storm of disruption. The maturity of ecommerce in the region has leapfrogged, with a decade’s worth of adoption 
taking place in just three years. The rapid rise of digital commerce is changing retailers’ objectives from making a sale to 
providing contextualized experiences to consumers, while the stores are transforming from “sales centers” into “experience 
centers.”
The key objectives of these transformation efforts reflect the sector’s overall transformation and the current economic 
climate. Improving customer experience, along with business continuity and revenue growth, are the most important focus 
areas for such initiatives. However, the focus on frontline workers — improving their productivity and overall experience 
— has taken a backseat to the above-mentioned objectives and needs to be given higher priority. A business function is 
only as good as the employees that perform it, and that includes the tools that enable them to do their jobs effectively. A 
productive and happy workforce is key to achieving organizational and functional goals.

CHALLENGES FACED BY FIELD WORKERS AND MANAGERS
Work transformation efforts often lack a critical component — feedback from frontline employees. IDC’s survey reveals that 
retail associates in South Africa face various challenges that affect their productivity and employee experience, such as 
hectic schedules, dull and repetitive tasks, and insufficient staff to perform duties. This is leading to increasing stress among 
employees, which needs to be addressed urgently as it approaches a peak. 
The survey findings emphasize the need to prioritize employee productivity and experience in work transformation efforts. 
Digital tools can help retailers vastly improve the quality of work and reduce stress among frontline employees through 
effective communication, resource allocation, and task scheduling. They can also free up employee time for meaningful 
work by automating repetitive and mundane tasks. Frontline employees and managers in South Africa’s retail sector both 
perceive technology as the most important enabler of work effectiveness, as indicated later in this study.

Managing workplace stress is a critical theme — one being discussed 
and addressed by industry-leading organizations across the globe. 
Such discussions often call for changes in organizational culture, 
spearheaded by technology-enabled workplace transformation. The 
criticality of improving employee productivity and managing employee 
stress and mental health is evident in IDC’s survey results: 60% of HR 
managers and many retail managers cited this as a key challenge.  
Retail organizations should focus on motivating their frontline staff by 
providing them with the autonomy and decision-making power they 
need to do their jobs effectively. Fostering a sense of ownership 
and accountability among retail associates can lead to increased 
engagement and motivation. Improving employee productivity and 
overall experience through the technological support of day-to-day 
task management, monitoring employee performance, and keeping 
employees motivated can also improve customer satisfaction and the 
overall efficiency and effectiveness of the business. 
Acquiring and developing skills is another key challenge highlighted 
by retail managers in South Africa. The high attrition among retail 
associates intensifies the skills challenge. Around 36% of the retail 
associates who participated in IDC’s survey indicated that they had 
changed jobs in the last two years. The skills issue is not restricted 
to retail associates and affects most industries and job functions in the 
country, making it a strategic focus area in national development. It 
is also a challenge that technology can address. Skills development 
through digitally enabled training and learning is a key technology use 
case highlighted by retail associates, as indicated later in this study.

EMPOWERING RETAIL ASSOCIATES WITH TECHNOLOGY
All business functions today are supported by digital tools that align with those functions to keep businesses agile in rapidly 
changing market conditions. However, retail associates have historically been underserved when it comes to employer-
provided workplace technology. In the early days of transitioning to a digitally driven workplace, frontline workflows did not 
fit the typical use cases and are only now becoming part of the future-of-work conversation. Business-class communications 
and collaboration capabilities on mobile devices or cloud-based meeting platforms are ideal to enhance productivity for 
retail associates. Most employees and managers feel that technology can significantly improve their effectiveness and 
engagement, thereby addressing one of their key challenges — managing stress and employee mental health (see Figure 4).
Nevertheless, the importance of technology as perceived by retail associates and 
managers is not reflected in the digital maturity of the function. Only a small percentage 
of organizations in the retail sector (9%) have all of their frontline processes digitized. 
For the majority of the organizations (60%), half or more of their processes remain paper 
based. Industrywide transformation efforts reveal that many organizations lag behind 
their peers. The majority of retail associates in South Africa (63%) believe they are only 
on par or below the industry average in terms of digital maturity, lacking any competitive 
advantage over their peers in this regard.
As mentioned earlier in this report, many retail organizations in the country are pursuing 
their frontline work transformation initiatives, driven by customer demand and industry-
wide transformation. To lay out an effective path to future transformation, it is critical to 
understand the status of these efforts and the current maturity of digital transformation. 
The results of this study show the importance of accelerating the transformation efforts 
with a focus on the right areas, such as employee productivity and experience.
